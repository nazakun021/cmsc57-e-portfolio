I. Counting

A. Basics of Counting

Reflection: Understanding basic counting helped me organize and evaluate situations where I need to find the number of possible outcomes.

Thoughts: It reminded me that even simple scenarios often involve combinatorial thinking.

Application: Determining the number of possible license plates or phone numbers.

Learnings: I learned about the product rule, the sum rule, and tree diagrams.

Analysis: These principles are foundational to all combinatorics problems we tackled later in the course.

B. Pigeonhole Principle

Reflection: It was surprising how simple logic can guarantee repetition in a set.

Thoughts: This topic made me appreciate the depth behind simple ideas.

Application: Proving that in any group of 367 people, at least two share a birthday.

Learnings: I learned that if you have more pigeons than pigeonholes, some must share.

Analysis: This concept is crucial in probability and optimization problems.

C. Permutation and Combination

Reflection: These tools are essential in problem-solving scenarios involving order or selection.

Thoughts: Differentiating when to use permutations versus combinations was tricky at first.

Application: Permutation: Arranging books on a shelf. Combination: Selecting a committee from a group of people.

Learnings: Order matters in permutations, not in combinations.

Analysis: These help break down large, complex scenarios into manageable math.

D. Generalized Permutations and Combinations

Reflection: Generalization expanded my ability to model more complex problems.

Thoughts: Factoring in repeated elements and constraints was a new challenge.

Application: Calculating the number of distinct arrangements of letters in a word with repeated letters (e.g., "MISSISSIPPI").

Learnings: Adjusting formulas to consider identical items is key.

Analysis: Crucial when modeling realistic scenarios like codes or arrangements.

E. Generating Permutations and Combinations

Reflection: Generating outcomes programmatically felt powerful.

Thoughts: I appreciated seeing algorithmic approaches to counting problems.

Application: Creating all possible lottery number combinations for analysis.

Learnings: Recursive and iterative methods can systematically solve such problems.

Analysis: Helps connect math concepts with practical coding skills.

II. Algebraic Structure and Groups

Reflection: Understanding algebraic structures changed the way I view mathematical systems.

Thoughts: I didn't expect groups to be so structured yet abstract.

Application: Cryptography, where group theory is used in algorithms like RSA and Diffie-Hellman.

Learnings: Learned about groups, identity elements, and inverses.

Analysis: Groups provide a framework for consistent operations across sets.

III. Groups, Rings, and Fields

Reflection: These structures built on previous concepts and introduced new depth.

Thoughts: The leap from groups to rings and fields made algebra feel more connected.

Application: Error correction codes used in data transmission and storage (e.g., CDs, QR codes).

Learnings: Rings allow two operations, fields allow division (excluding zero).

Analysis: These systems are essential for computer science and coding theory.

IV. Discrete Probability

A. Introduction to Discrete Probability

Reflection: Helped me think in terms of likelihood rather than certainty.

Thoughts: Probability complements counting perfectly.

Application: Assessing the likelihood of rain to plan outdoor activities.

Learnings: Probability is about favorable over total outcomes.

Analysis: Foundational in decision making under uncertainty.

B. Probability Theory

Reflection: Expanded my thinking from single events to compound and conditional events.

Thoughts: Venn diagrams really helped visualize it.

Application: Insurance companies use it to determine premium rates based on risk assessment.

Learnings: Concepts of union, intersection, and independence.

Analysis: Powerful tool for understanding interconnected systems.

C. Bayes Theorem

Reflection: The idea of updating beliefs with new information fascinated me.

Thoughts: Bayes' Theorem is very applicable to real-world decision-making.

Application: Spam filters in email clients use it to classify emails as spam or not spam.

Learnings: Learned how to use conditional probability to update chances.

Analysis: Helps with decision-making in the presence of prior knowledge.

D. Expected Value and Variance

Reflection: These taught me how to anticipate outcomes and variability.

Thoughts: Understanding variance made me value stability in systems.

Application: Used in financial analysis to determine the potential return and risk of an investment.

Learnings: Expected value gives an average outcome; variance shows spread.

Analysis: Essential for probability-based decisions in finance and games.

V. Graphs

A. Graphs and Graph Models

Reflection: Graphs helped visualize complex relationships.

Thoughts: Found it cool how they relate to social networks and maps.

Application: Social networks like Facebook or LinkedIn model connections between users.

Learnings: Vertices represent items, edges represent relationships.

Analysis: Graph theory is central to computer networks and algorithms.

B. Graph Terminology and Special Types of Graphs

Reflection: Learning terminology helped communicate graph ideas better.

Thoughts: Special types of graphs make specific problems easier.

Application: Modeling chemical compounds where atoms are vertices and bonds are edges.

Learnings: Terms like degree, path, cycle are fundamental.

Analysis: Clear vocabulary helps when developing graph-based algorithms.

C. Representing Graphs and Graph Isomorphism

Reflection: Representation changed how I viewed the same graph in different forms.

Thoughts: Graph isomorphism showed how structure matters more than appearance.

Application: Verifying if two different circuit layouts are functionally identical.

Learnings: Different representations offer different strengths.

Analysis: Isomorphism helps in recognizing similar patterns in systems.

D. Connectivity

Reflection: Connectivity relates directly to network resilience.

Thoughts: Strong and weak connections define how robust systems are.

Application: Analyzing the reliability of communication networks (e.g., internet, phone networks).

Learnings: Connected graphs ensure every vertex can reach another.

Analysis: Important for fault-tolerant systems.

E. Euler and Hamiltonian Paths

Reflection: These classic problems introduced me to graph traversal strategies.

Thoughts: Euler vs. Hamiltonian paths helped distinguish between edge and vertex coverage.

Application: Planning routes for mail delivery or garbage collection to cover all streets efficiently (Euler path).

Learnings: Euler covers edges once; Hamiltonian covers vertices once.

Analysis: These are vital for routing and scheduling problems.

F. Shortest-Path Problem

Reflection: Optimizing paths revealed the power of algorithms like Dijkstra's.

Thoughts: Pathfinding made me appreciate efficient algorithms.

Application: GPS navigation systems finding the quickest route between two locations.

Learnings: Algorithms like BFS, Dijkstra find optimal routes.

Analysis: Central to GPS systems and network packet routing.

G. Planar Graphs

Reflection: Planarity introduced a visual and geometric aspect to graph theory.

Thoughts: Kuratowski's Theorem was particularly interesting.

Application: Designing printed circuit boards (PCBs) to avoid crossing connections.

Learnings: Planar graphs can be drawn without edge crossings.

Analysis: Planarity matters in reducing complexity and cost in design.

H. Graph Coloring

Reflection: Coloring problems were like solving logic puzzles.

Thoughts: I was surprised how hard it is to color graphs with minimal colors.

Application: Scheduling exams so that no student has two exams at the same time.

Learnings: Each adjacent vertex must be a different color.

Analysis: Crucial in resource allocation and conflict resolution scenarios.
